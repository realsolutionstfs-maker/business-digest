[TIMECODE 0:30-3:00] — ACT I: THE RISE
Hastings founded Netflix in 1997 after a late fee on a VHS rental. The original idea: DVD-by-mail.

They launched in 1998 with 30 employees and 925 titles.

[SOURCE: Wikipedia]

[TIMECODE 3:00-7:00] — ACT II: THE STRUGGLE
The first near-death moment: 2000. Netflix had 300,000 subscribers. It was losing money on every one of them. Postage costs were destroying margins.

[PAUSE — 1s]

Then came the dot-com crash. Netflix's stock dropped 90%.

[SOURCE: That Will Never Work]

Second near-death moment: 2007. The pivot from DVDs to streaming. The technology didn't exist. The content deals didn't exist. The bandwidth didn't exist.

Third near-death moment: 2011. Qwikster. Hastings announced Netflix would split into two companies. DVDs would be Qwikster. Streaming would be Netflix. Price would increase 60%.

[PAUSE — 2s]

800,000 subscribers quit in one quarter. Stock dropped 80%.

[SOURCE: Qwikster crisis analysis]

[TIMECODE 7:00-9:30] — ACT III: THE RESOLUTION
Hastings apologized. Canceled Qwikster. The company recovered.

Went all-in on original content in 2013. House of Cards cost $100 million for two seasons.

[PAUSE — 1s]

By 2023, Netflix had 260 million subscribers. $33 billion in revenue.

[TIMECODE 9:30-10:00] — TAKEAWAY
The lesson: cannibalize yourself before someone else does. [EMPHASIS: Cannibalize yourself before someone else does.] Netflix killed its own DVD business to build streaming. Then it killed licensed content to build originals.

Your biggest competitor is your current business model.
