Adam Neumann walked onto a stage in 2017, barefoot, and told 5,000 employees that WeWork's purpose was to "elevate the world's consciousness."

[PAUSE — 1s]

One year later, the company was worth $47 billion.

Seven weeks after that, it was worth zero.

[TIMECODE 0:30-3:00] — ACT I: THE RISE
Neumann started WeWork in 2010. The idea was simple: lease office space, subdivide it, rent it to freelancers and startups.

By 2014, WeWork had 20 locations. By 2017, it had 280.

[SOURCE: Wikipedia]

[TIMECODE 3:00-7:00] — ACT II: THE STRUGGLE
The problem was hidden in plain sight. WeWork's model required occupancy rates above 80% to be profitable. Most locations ran at 60-70%.

[SOURCE: SEC S-1 filing]

Meanwhile, Neumann was buying buildings with WeWork money — then leasing them back to WeWork at a profit. He trademarked the word "We." He received $5.9 million for the rights.

[PAUSE — 2s]

The S-1 filing in August 2019 revealed all of this.

[TIMECODE 7:00-9:30] — ACT III: THE RESOLUTION
The IPO was pulled. SoftBank wrote down its $10 billion investment. Neumann was ousted with a $1.7 billion exit package.

WeWork filed for bankruptcy in 2023.

[SOURCE: WSJ investigation]

[TIMECODE 9:30-10:00] — TAKEAWAY
The lesson: revenue is not a business model. [EMPHASIS: Revenue is not a business model.] WeWork had revenue. It never had a profitable unit.

Before you scale, prove one unit works.
