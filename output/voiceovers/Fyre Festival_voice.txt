In April 2017, the first guests arrived at Fyre Festival. They found disaster-relief tents. Cheese sandwiches in styrofoam containers. And no music.

[PAUSE — 1s]

They had paid between 1,500 and 250,000 dollars per ticket.

Billy McFarland was 25 years old. He had already built a successful company called Magnises. A black card for millennials.

His next idea: a luxury music festival on a private island in the Bahamas.

Here is what actually happened. McFarland hired a team. They had six weeks to organize what normally takes 18 months.

[PAUSE — 1s]

They booked Villa Castella. A three-thousand-dollar-per-night resort. It became the staff headquarters. But the festival was supposed to be on Great Exuma. A different island. Where no infrastructure existed.

They spent seven million dollars on influencer marketing. Kendall Jenner. Bella Hadid. Models on yachts in matching orange Fyre-branded swimwear.

They spent zero dollars on stage infrastructure.

[PAUSE — 2s]

The ticket revenue: 26 million dollars. The actual cost to execute: 50 million dollars.

McFarland was charged with wire fraud. Sentenced to six years in federal prison.

The lesson: marketing cannot replace operations. Fyre executed the perfect launch. They had zero execution.

Sell what you can deliver. Not what you wish you could deliver.
