In 2000, Reed Hastings flew to Dallas to beg Blockbuster to buy Netflix for 50 million dollars.

Blockbuster said no.

[PAUSE — 2s]

Today, Blockbuster has one store. Netflix is worth 250 billion dollars.

Hastings founded Netflix in 1997 after a late fee on a VHS rental. The original idea: DVD-by-mail.

They launched in 1998 with 30 employees and 925 titles.

The first near-death moment: 2000. Netflix had 300,000 subscribers. It was losing money on every one of them. Postage costs were destroying margins.

Then came the dot-com crash. Netflix stock dropped 90 percent.

[PAUSE — 1s]

Second near-death moment: 2007. The pivot from DVDs to streaming. The technology did not exist. The content deals did not exist. The bandwidth did not exist.

Third near-death moment: 2011. Qwikster. Hastings announced Netflix would split into two companies. Price would increase 60 percent.

Eight hundred thousand subscribers quit in one quarter. Stock dropped 80 percent.

[PAUSE — 2s]

Hastings apologized. Canceled Qwikster. The company recovered.

Went all-in on original content in 2013. House of Cards cost 100 million dollars for two seasons.

By 2023, Netflix had 260 million subscribers. 33 billion dollars in revenue.

The lesson: cannibalize yourself before someone else does. Netflix killed its own DVD business to build streaming. Then it killed licensed content to build originals.

Your biggest competitor is your current business model.
