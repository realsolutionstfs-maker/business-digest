Elizabeth Holmes dropped out of Stanford at 19. She had an idea: a machine that could run hundreds of blood tests from a single finger prick.

She called it Theranos. Short for "therapy" and "diagnosis."

Here is the thing. By 2013, Theranos was valued at nine billion dollars. Holmes had raised seven hundred million dollars from investors like Rupert Murdoch and the Walton family.

But there was a problem. The machine did not work.

[PAUSE — 2s]

Former employees later testified that the team ran patient blood samples on modified Siemens machines. Not the proprietary Edison device they had promised.

In 2018, the SEC charged Theranos with fraud. Holmes was banned from serving as an officer of a public company for ten years.

In 2022, she was sentenced to 11 years in federal prison.

[PAUSE — 1s]

A nine billion dollar company. Zero working technology.

The lesson: process beats vision. Holmes had the vision. What she lacked was a mechanism to verify whether the technology actually worked.
