Adam Neumann walked onto a stage in 2017, barefoot, and told 5,000 employees that WeWork's purpose was to elevate the world's consciousness.

[PAUSE — 1s]

One year later, the company was worth 47 billion dollars.

Seven weeks after that, it was worth zero.

Neumann started WeWork in 2010. The idea was simple: lease office space, subdivide it, rent it to freelancers and startups.

By 2014, WeWork had 20 locations. By 2017, it had 280.

Here is the problem. It was hidden in plain sight. WeWork's model required occupancy rates above 80 percent to be profitable. Most locations ran at 60 to 70 percent.

Meanwhile, Neumann was buying buildings with WeWork money. Then leasing them back to WeWork at a profit. He trademarked the word "We." He received 5.9 million dollars for the rights.

[PAUSE — 2s]

The S-1 filing in August 2019 revealed all of this.

The IPO was pulled. SoftBank wrote down its ten billion dollar investment. Neumann was ousted with a 1.7 billion dollar exit package.

WeWork filed for bankruptcy in 2023.

The lesson: revenue is not a business model. WeWork had revenue. It never had a profitable unit.

Before you scale, prove one unit works.
